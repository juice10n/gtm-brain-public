# Company Assessment Template

**Purpose:** Structured intake framework for evaluating a company's GTM motion. Used in interview mode — assessing a company before joining, demonstrating GTM depth to founders/boards, and building a 90-day plan that shows how you think.

**How it works:** Collect company facts first, then assess tenet-by-tenet, then run the red flag scan, then synthesize cross-tenet. Output is a GTM assessment, 90-day plan, or gap identification depending on context.

---

## Company Profile (collect first)

| Field | What to capture |
|---|---|
| ARR / Revenue | Current revenue, growth rate, trajectory |
| ACV | Average deal size, range, trend direction |
| Team | Sales team size, structure, tenure. Marketing? CS? Ops? RevOps? |
| Product | What it does, who it's for, maturity stage, technical architecture |
| Funding / Stage | Series, runway, investor expectations, board composition |
| Current Motion | Inbound, outbound, PLG, partner, referral — what's working, what's not |
| Customer Count | Total customers, logo retention |
| NRR / Churn | Net revenue retention, gross retention, churn rate by segment. A company with 200 logos and 85% NRR is a fundamentally different situation than one with 200 logos and 115% NRR — this shapes the entire GTM diagnosis. |
| Sales Cycle | Average days to close, by segment if applicable |
| Win Rate | Overall and by stage if available |
| CRM & Tech Stack | CRM system and data quality. Marketing automation, intent data, enrichment, sales engagement tooling. What's connected, what's shelfware. |
| Competitive Landscape | Who they lose to, who they get compared to, what category they compete in. This shapes positioning (Tenet 2), messaging (Tenet 5), and pricing (Tenet 8) downstream. |
| Content & Brand | Website quality, content engine maturity, brand awareness in target market. Is there anything to build on or is it net-new? |

---

## Scoring Scale

| Level | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Instinct | Opinions but no system. Lives in someone's head. |
| 2 | Practiced | Done it at least once. May be company-specific. |
| 3 | Systematic | Repeatable framework. Can adapt to different contexts. |
| 4 | Architected | Can design from scratch. Knows failure modes and edge cases. |

For each score below 4: **Is this a ceiling or a circumstance?** Ceiling = genuinely at this level. Circumstance = depth may be higher but company stage didn't demand it.

## Red Flag Scan

Run this scan across the full company profile before diving into tenet-by-tenet assessment. These cross-tenet patterns often matter more than any individual tenet score — they indicate structural problems that can't be solved by fixing one tenet in isolation.

| Red Flag | What It Signals | Tenet Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Founder-led sales with no transition plan** | The GTM motion is one person's relationships, not a repeatable system. Every tenet scores artificially high because the founder compensates with context and credibility that won't transfer. | All tenets — but especially 7 (Process), 10 (Enablement), 4 (Discovery) |
| **VP Sales hired before ICP is defined** | The company hired a closer before knowing who to close. Expect high activity, low win rates, and a team selling to anyone who picks up the phone. | 1 (ICP), 6 (Outbound), 9 (Metrics) |
| **Series B+ with no documented sales process** | The company scaled headcount without scaling the operating system. Individual heroics got them here; process will get them to the next stage — or the lack of it will kill them. | 7 (Process), 9 (Metrics), 10 (Enablement) |
| **High logo count but flat or declining NRR** | The company can land deals but can't expand or retain. Either the product doesn't deliver ongoing value, the ICP is wrong (landing customers who shouldn't be customers), or there's no post-sale motion. | 1 (ICP), 3 (Buyer Journey), 8 (Pricing) |
| **Multiple product lines with one pitch** | The company built features for different buyers but still tells one story. Discovery doesn't segment, messaging doesn't adapt, and the champion hears the same deck as the CFO. | 2 (Value Prop), 4 (Discovery), 5 (Messaging) |
| **Heavy discounting as default close behavior** | The sales team doesn't believe in the price, or they don't know how to sell value. Either way, the cost-of-alternative anchor is missing and the pricing model may not be aligned to value delivery. | 8 (Pricing), 2 (Value Prop), 4 (Discovery) |
| **CRM is a graveyard** | Nobody trusts the data, pipeline reviews are guesswork, and forecast accuracy is a coin flip. You can't diagnose anything downstream if the measurement layer is broken. | 9 (Metrics), 7 (Process) |
| **Revolving door in sales leadership** | Two or more VP Sales in the last 3 years. Either the company has unrealistic expectations, the product-market fit isn't there, or the CEO won't let go of founder-led selling. Diagnose which before accepting the role. | All tenets — this is a company-level red flag, not a tenet-level one |
| **Outbound is the only channel and it's not working** | No inbound engine, no content, no brand. If outbound stalls, pipeline goes to zero. The company needs a channel diversification strategy, not just better sequences. | 6 (Outbound), 2 (Value Prop) |
| **"We just need more pipeline"** | The most common misdiagnosis in B2B. Sometimes the problem is pipeline. More often it's win rate, deal velocity, ICP clarity, or pricing. If the company's instinct is always "more at-bats," the real problem is downstream. | 1 (ICP), 4 (Discovery), 8 (Pricing), 9 (Metrics) |

**How to use:** If 3+ red flags are present, the company likely needs a foundational rebuild (Days 1–30 of the 90-day plan becomes purely diagnostic). If 1–2 are present, they become the priority in the cross-tenet synthesis. If none are present, the company is probably further along than most and the work is optimization, not construction.

## Tenet-by-Tenet Assessment

### Tenet 1 — ICP & Market Segmentation

**Intake:** Who are you selling to today? What use cases are winning? Which deals are you losing and why? Have you done any structured exercise to disaggregate personas across your customer base? What does your negative ICP look like — who should you stop selling to?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "You have 40 customers but no clear pattern — walk me through who's succeeding vs. churning."
- "How do you decide when to go upmarket vs. stay in your lane?"
- "You sell to both X and Y teams. If you could only focus on one for two quarters, which and why?"

**Score on:** ICP clarity, persona disaggregation, use-case specificity, negative ICP awareness, refinement cadence.

**What good looks like:** The best companies can articulate not just who they sell to, but why those customers succeed — and which customers they should stop pursuing. A structured stakeholder mapping exercise applied across the customer base produces ICP, positioning inputs, and messaging angles simultaneously. The negative ICP is documented and actively used in pipeline reviews to prune early. Continuous refinement happens through win/loss analysis and churn pattern review, not annual strategy offsites.

**Reference patterns:** Stakeholder mapping as a facilitated exercise (not just a template), use-case-first segmentation rather than firmographic-first, upmarket shift signal sets, inherited customer base diagnosis when joining a company mid-flight.

---

### Tenet 2 — Value Proposition & Positioning

**Intake:** How do you describe what you do in one sentence? What category do you compete in — is it the right one? What do you get compared to? What's your cost-of-alternative anchor? Can you articulate what you do better than anyone else today?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Your best customers love you for different reasons. How do you reconcile that into one story?"
- "How do you know when your positioning is wrong? What are the leading indicators?"
- "If I gave you 60 seconds to explain why a prospect should take a meeting, what would you say?"

**Score on:** Category clarity, differentiation specificity, cost-of-alternative anchoring, intro deck narrative arc, competitive positioning approach.

**What good looks like:** The company has a clear category — either they compete in an existing one with sharp differentiation, or they've created a new category that changes how buyers think about the problem. Positioning isn't just a tagline; it shows up in how the intro deck flows, how discovery questions are framed, and how pricing is anchored. The cost-of-alternative calculation is specific and quantified, not hand-wavy. When competitive comparisons come up, the response is a fork ("they're great at X, we're built for Y") not a feature-by-feature comparison.

**Reference patterns:** Category creation when the existing category undersells the product, cost-of-alternative decks that anchor price to current spend (not to competitor pricing), competitive positioning through forking rather than fighting.

---

### Tenet 3 — Buyer Journey & Sales Motion

**Intake:** Walk me through how a deal moves from first touch to closed. Where do deals stall? Is there a technical validation step separate from business validation? Does the buyer know how to buy your product?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Your sales cycle is X days. Is that good or bad? How would you compress it?"
- "You lose a lot of deals after the second call. What would you look at?"
- "What's your view on pilots vs. going straight to annual contracts?"

**Score on:** Stage architecture clarity, stall pattern awareness, buyer-side journey understanding, technical/business validation separation.

**What good looks like:** The company distinguishes between its sales process (what the seller does) and the buyer's journey (what the buyer needs to believe, decide, and do at each stage). Stall patterns are diagnosed by stage, not just by vibes. Technical validation and business validation are treated as separate gates with different stakeholders. The landing motion (pilot vs. POC vs. straight-to-annual) is a deliberate choice with clear selection criteria, not a default. The buyer's champion has been armed with materials to navigate their own internal process — procurement, legal, IT security — because deals die in the buyer's organization, not in the seller's pipeline.

**Reference patterns:** Six-step motion build process for new companies (diagnose before installing), pilot-as-landing-motion with defined success criteria, buyer enablement artifacts that help the champion sell internally, buyer-side journey mapping as a distinct discipline from seller-side process design.

---

### Tenet 4 — Discovery & Qualification

**Intake:** What does your first call look like? What do you need to know before you demo? How do you decide a deal is real vs. tire-kicker? What are your disqualification criteria? Do reps run discovery or jump to demo?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "How do you teach a team to qualify without making every call feel like an interrogation?"
- "Lots of pipeline but low win rates. Where do you start diagnosing?"
- "How do you know when to disqualify? What's the hardest part about that?"

**Score on:** Discovery sequence, qualification signal clarity, disqualification discipline, intentional curiosity vs. checklist approach.

**What good looks like:** Discovery follows a deliberate sequence — context before pain, pain before impact, impact before solution — and reps understand why the order matters. Qualification signals are defined and observable, not just "BANT" or "MEDDPICC" as a checklist. The team has explicit disqualification criteria — a short list of signals that mean "stop pursuing this deal" — and the discipline to use them. The discovery philosophy is intentional curiosity, not interrogation: the prospect should feel like they're in a valuable conversation, not an intake form.

**Reference patterns:** Sequenced discovery (5+ step models that build context before proposing solutions), negative ICP checklists with specific kill criteria, objection handling reframed as deeper discovery rather than overcoming resistance, qualification frameworks that function as decision logic rather than acronym checklists.

---

### Tenet 5 — Messaging by Persona

**Intake:** Does your pitch change by persona? How do you talk to a practitioner vs. their VP vs. procurement? What proof points do you have and where do you deploy them? Can your champion articulate your value when you're not in the room?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Your champion loves you but can't get budget approval. What's your playbook?"
- "You sell to 4 personas in the buying committee. How do you build messaging for each without feeling like 4 different products?"
- "What's the difference between a champion and an internal coach?"

**Score on:** Persona-level language adaptation, proof point deployment specificity, champion arming capability, executive escalation approach.

**What good looks like:** The pitch shifts by persona — same product, different emphasis, different language, different proof points. Practitioners hear workflow impact; their VP hears team efficiency and risk reduction; the CFO hears cost-of-alternative and ROI. Proof points are deployed strategically: the right metric for the right persona at the right stage of the deal. The champion has been given specific language and materials to advocate internally — not just "here's a one-pager" but "here's exactly what to say to your VP when they ask why this matters."

**Reference patterns:** Language shift tables (documenting how the pitch changes by persona and deal stage), proof point libraries indexed by persona and objection, champion arming as a deliberate step in the sales process, executive escalation playbooks that connect practitioner-level value to executive-level initiatives.

---

### Tenet 6 — Outbound & Demand Gen

**Intake:** How are you generating pipeline today? Inbound, outbound, referral, PLG, partner? What's working and what's not? If outbound, what's your sequence structure, channel mix, and tooling? Do you have intent data or trigger-based targeting?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Build pipeline from zero. No brand, no inbound. What do you do in the first 90 days?"
- "When do you hire SDRs vs. have AEs self-source?"
- "You tried outbound sequences and got a 2% reply rate. What would you change?"

**Score on:** Channel strategy clarity, sequence design sophistication, tooling maturity, attribution approach, pipeline creation vs. capture distinction.

**What good looks like:** The company distinguishes between pipeline creation (generating demand that didn't exist) and pipeline capture (converting demand that already exists). Channel strategy is deliberate — the company knows which channels produce quality pipeline, not just volume. Outbound sequences are designed with signal-based triggers (job changes, funding events, technology adoption) rather than spray-and-pray. The SDR vs. AE self-sourcing decision is based on deal complexity and ACV, not just "we need more meetings." Attribution exists and is trusted enough to inform channel investment decisions.

**Reference patterns:** Three-layer pipeline philosophy (network seeding → outbound → inbound/content), signal-triggered outbound frameworks, SDR decision trees based on ACV and motion type, channel selection diagnostics that match channel to buyer behavior.

---

### Tenet 7 — Sales Process & Playbook

**Intake:** What are your deal stages? Are there exit criteria for each stage? How do you forecast? What does your pipeline review look like? Is the process documented or in someone's head?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "How do you build a sales process from scratch vs. adopting an existing framework?"
- "3 AEs who all sell differently. How do you standardize without killing what makes each effective?"
- "Deal stalls at Stage 3. What's your diagnostic framework?"

**Score on:** Stage architecture, exit criteria rigor, forecast methodology, pipeline review quality, documentation completeness.

**What good looks like:** Deal stages have defined exit criteria — not just "demo completed" but "the prospect has confirmed [specific thing] and we have [specific evidence]." Forecasting uses multiple lenses (commit, best case, pipeline) with different assumptions for each. Pipeline reviews are diagnostic, not just status updates — they identify why deals are stuck, not just that they are. The process is documented and a new rep could follow it without shadowing someone for three months. The CRM reflects reality because the process is designed to serve the seller, not just management reporting.

**Reference patterns:** Stage architecture with exit criteria and inspection questions at each gate, forecast methodology that accounts for deal type (pilot vs. direct), pipeline review as coaching opportunity rather than accountability theater, sales process built from observed deal patterns rather than imposed from a template, process enforcement through CRM design rather than compliance mandates.

---

### Tenet 8 — Pricing & Packaging

**Intake:** What's your pricing model? How did you arrive at it? What's your average deal size? How do you introduce pricing in the sales cycle? Do you offer pilots? How do you handle discounting pressure?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "You're per-seat today. Thinking about usage-based. What breaks when you make that switch?"
- "How do you set pricing with 30 customers and no real competitive benchmark?"
- "How do you think about packaging tiers? What determines whether you need two or four?"

**Score on:** Pricing model clarity, value metric identification, packaging logic, pilot structure, discounting discipline, pricing introduction timing.

**What good looks like:** The pricing model is aligned to how the buyer experiences value — the value metric matches the unit the buyer cares about. Pricing is introduced with confidence, not apologetically, and anchored to cost-of-alternative rather than to competitor pricing. Pilots have defined success criteria, timelines, and conversion paths — they're not indefinite trials. Discounting has guardrails: the team knows the floor, the triggers for escalation, and how to trade concessions (longer term, faster signature, case study rights) rather than just cutting price. Packaging tiers correspond to genuine buyer segments, not artificial feature gating.

**Reference patterns:** Pilot-first pricing with defined conversion mechanics, cost-of-alternative anchoring that makes the price feel inevitable, conviction selling (holding price because you've proven value), value metric selection frameworks, packaging that scales with the buyer's maturity (crawl/walk/run or activate/adopt/accelerate).

---

### Tenet 9 — Metrics & Pipeline Hygiene

**Intake:** What metrics do you track? What does the CEO see weekly? Conversion rates by stage? Win rate? Average sales cycle? Pipeline coverage ratio? Do you trust your CRM data?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "What metrics go on a weekly dashboard for me? Why those and not others?"
- "How do you know when pipeline coverage is real vs. stuffed with dead deals?"
- "You missed forecast by 30% last quarter. Walk me through diagnosing what went wrong."

**Score on:** Metric selection quality, dashboard design, forecast accuracy, CRM hygiene standards, post-mortem discipline.

**What good looks like:** The weekly dashboard is tight — 5–7 metrics that tell the CEO whether the engine is working, not 30 data points that create noise. Pipeline coverage is calculated with quality filters (deals with activity in the last 14 days, deals past a meaningful qualification threshold) not raw pipeline divided by target. Win/loss analysis happens systematically, not just when someone asks. Forecast accuracy is tracked as a meta-metric — the company knows whether its predictions are getting better or worse. CRM hygiene standards are defined and enforced through process design, not through nagging.

**Reference patterns:** SQO-anchored dashboards (measuring pipeline quality at the point where qualification is real), pipeline coverage models with quality filters, three-tier forecasting (commit/best case/pipeline) with historical accuracy tracking, deal post-mortem templates and quarterly closed-lost reviews, diagnostic frameworks that connect metric movements to upstream tenet breakdowns.

---

### Tenet 10 — Enablement & Onboarding

**Intake:** How do new reps ramp? How long to first deal? Is the playbook documented or tribal? What training materials exist? Who coaches reps on deals?

**Follow-up probes:**
- "Hiring 3 AEs next quarter. What does your onboarding plan look like?"
- "How do you keep a playbook alive? Every company I've been at, it gets stale in 6 months."
- "You're inheriting 5 reps, 2 not hitting quota. What's your first 30 days?"

**Score on:** Onboarding structure, ramp timeline, playbook documentation, coaching cadence, knowledge transfer methodology.

**What good looks like:** New reps have a structured 30/60/90 plan with defined readiness gates — not just "shadow calls for a month." The playbook exists as a living document, not a slide deck that was built once and forgotten. Coaching is a regular cadence (weekly deal reviews, call listening), not an event triggered by poor performance. Knowledge transfer is systematized so the methodology doesn't walk out the door when one person leaves. Ramp time is measured and benchmarked — the company knows whether it's getting better at onboarding.

**Reference patterns:** Readiness-based ramp (milestone gates, not time-based promotion), multi-mode coaching (Socratic questioning for developing judgment, directive coaching for urgent corrections, reinforcement for embedding habits), minimum viable enablement artifacts (the 5 things a new rep needs on day one), playbook reinforcement cadences that keep content alive, knowledge transfer as a deliberate system rather than tribal knowledge.

## Cross-Tenet Synthesis

After scoring all 10 tenets and running the red flag scan, synthesize:

**1. What's the biggest constraint?** The 1–2 tenets that are most broken and most blocking everything else. A weak ICP poisons positioning, messaging, outbound, and pipeline quality. A weak sales process means nothing downstream is consistent. Name the constraint, don't list all 10 problems.

**2. What's actually working?** Name what shouldn't be touched. Identify the tenets at Level 3+ that should be preserved, not rebuilt.

**3. Where does fixing one thing unlock others?** The tenets are a system. Fixing ICP clarity might automatically improve win rates, reduce discounting, and sharpen outbound. Find the leverage point — the one action that creates the most downstream improvement.

**4. Methodology match — where does existing capability create immediate leverage?**

Map the company's biggest constraints against available expertise. Three categories:

**Immediate depth:** Tenets where proven, transferable methodology exists and the company's constraint matches. These are Day 1 priorities — start building within the first week because the framework is ready and the company needs it now.

**Methodology with infrastructure to build:** Tenets where the strategic thinking is sound and transferable but the company-specific implementation needs to be constructed. Diagnose first, then build — the frameworks exist, the company-specific application is the work.

**Build alongside the company:** Tenets where the approach would need to be developed in partnership with the company's existing team, data, and context. Define the specific learning plan: who to talk to, what to study, what the first deliverable looks like, and the timeline.

**5. Do the red flags change the picture?** If red flags were identified in the scan above, revisit the tenet scores through that lens. A company with a revolving door in sales leadership might score well on individual tenets (because each VP Sales built something) but the real problem is organizational — and fixing it requires a different conversation than tenet-by-tenet improvement.

## Output Formats

### GTM Assessment

**Structure:**
- Company profile summary (one paragraph)
- Red flag scan results (if any)
- Scorecard — 10 tenets, one line each: score + one-sentence assessment
- Deep dives on the 3–4 tenets that matter most for this company
- Cross-tenet synthesis: biggest constraint, what's working, leverage point, methodology fit
- Prioritized recommendations, numbered, in order of impact

### 90-Day Plan

**Structure:**
- Days 1–30: Diagnose + quick wins — what to learn, what to fix immediately, which red flags to address first
- Days 31–60: Build the foundation — the 1–2 highest-leverage systems to construct
- Days 61–90: Operate and prove — run the motion, produce early results, validate the approach
- Each action tagged to the tenet it addresses
- Each action has a concrete deliverable, not just a direction
- For areas requiring learning: specific learning plan with sources, timeline, and first deliverable

### Gap Identification

**Structure:**
- Critical gaps — will hurt the company if not addressed in 90 days
- Structural gaps — need to be built but won't kill the company short-term
- Optimization opportunities — working but could be better
- Methodology match — where existing frameworks create immediate leverage vs. where the approach would need to be built alongside the company's specific context

---

*This template is the interface between a static playbook and a dynamic company evaluation. Company facts are the inputs, tenet assessment is the diagnostic, red flag scan is the pattern recognition layer, and the output format is the deliverable. The methodology match in the cross-tenet synthesis is what makes the assessment prescriptive — it connects the diagnosis to a specific build plan.*
