## The Operating Principle

The outreach to a self-serve account is fundamentally different from cold outbound. You are not introducing yourself — you are acknowledging a relationship that already exists. The product data is your credibility. Every touch should reference what you know about their usage. "I noticed your team…" not "I'd love to introduce you to…"

The first touch is not a discovery call in the traditional sense. The rep already has behavioral discovery from the product data. The conversation deepens organizational context — how the product fits into their stack, who else cares, what the roadmap looks like — not pain qualification. The pain is validated by their usage.

## Scenario 1: Tier 1 Harvest

**When to use:** The account has crossed the PQA threshold — enterprise-ready behavioral signals, Stakeholder Framework personas are reachable, tier placement is Harvest. This is proactive sales outreach to the highest-value accounts.

**Goal:** Initiate a conversation about enterprise commitment. Move the account from self-serve consumption to an active sales engagement (S0 → S1 in the PLG pipeline).

### Trigger-Specific First Touch

The engagement trigger determines the opening frame. A billing inquiry gets a different first touch than a usage velocity signal because the buyer's mindset is different.

**Trigger: Spend threshold crossed (e.g., $1K+/month sustained 60+ days)**

> Subject: Your team's [Product] usage

> Hi [Name],

> I work with our larger accounts to help them get more value from [Product] — things like volume pricing, dedicated support, and custom configurations.

> Your team's usage has grown steadily over the past few months, and I wanted to reach out because most teams at your scale find that a committed agreement saves them 15-25% versus pay-as-you-go while adding [SLA / support / enterprise features they don't currently have].

> Would it make sense to spend 20 minutes exploring whether that's relevant for where your team is headed?

**Trigger: Multiple users from same corporate domain (team spread)**

> Subject: [Company name] + [Product]

> Hi [Name],

> I noticed several people on the [Company] team are using [Product] — it looks like it's moved beyond a single-user tool into something your team relies on.

> I work with our accounts where that kind of team adoption is happening to make sure the setup matches how your organization wants to use us — things like team-level administration, shared configurations, and volume pricing.

> Is there a good time this week to connect? I'd love to understand how your team is using [Product] and whether a more structured relationship makes sense.

**Trigger: Billing inquiry or invoice request**

> Subject: Re: Your billing request

> Hi [Name],

> I saw your team's billing inquiry come through and wanted to reach out directly. When an account requests invoicing, it usually means your organization is thinking about [Product] as a formal vendor relationship rather than an individual tool — and that's exactly the kind of conversation I help with.

> I can walk you through our committed pricing structure, which typically saves 15-25% versus pay-as-you-go, and make sure your team has everything procurement needs — SOC 2, security documentation, standard contract.

> Can we find 20 minutes this week?

**Trigger: Sandbox-to-production transition**

> Subject: Your production deployment on [Product]

> Hi [Name],

> I noticed your team recently moved to production workloads on [Product] — that's a meaningful signal that you're building real infrastructure on top of us.

> Most teams at this stage start thinking about availability guarantees, SLAs, and the difference between self-serve support and having a dedicated point of contact. I work with our production accounts to make sure the relationship structure matches the criticality of the workload.

> Worth a quick conversation?

### First Call Talk Track (Tier 1)

The rep arrives informed. The call is not "tell me about your business" — it is "I can see how your team uses us, help me understand the bigger picture."

**Opening (2 min):**
"Thanks for making time. I've been looking at how your team uses [Product] — [reference specific data: number of users, usage pattern, growth trend]. I work with our larger accounts to help them get more value, and your usage pattern looks similar to other organizations where a committed relationship made sense. I'd like to understand the bigger picture — how [Product] fits into your infrastructure and whether a more structured relationship is relevant."

**Organizational context (10 min) — the human conversation adds value here:**
- "How does [Product] fit into your broader infrastructure / tech stack / workflow?"
- "Who else on your team is involved in decisions about tools like this?"
- "Where do you see usage going over the next 6-12 months? Are there other teams or use cases you'd want to expand into?"
- "Is there a budget cycle or planning process that would affect timing?"

**Bridge to commitment (5 min):**
"Based on what you're telling me, a committed agreement would [specific value: save X%, add SLA, provide dedicated support, give admin controls]. Here's what that typically looks like for an account at your scale: [brief pricing frame — not a proposal, a frame]. Does that range feel relevant?"

**Close (3 min):**
"The next step would be [specific: send you a usage summary showing your team's adoption, put together a cost comparison between your current pay-as-you-go and a committed rate, schedule a call with your [VP/CTO] to discuss]. Which of those makes the most sense?"

### Follow-Up Sequence (Tier 1)

| Touch | Timing | Channel | Content |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Day 0 | Email | First touch (trigger-specific template above) |
| 2 | Day 3 | Email | Follow-up if no response: shorter, adds one new data point about their usage |
| 3 | Day 7 | LinkedIn | Connect request with brief note referencing their team's usage |
| 4 | Day 10 | Email | Value-add: send a relevant customer example or usage insight, not another ask |
| 5 | Day 14 | Email | Direct ask: "Is this relevant, or should I check back in a few months?" Give them an off-ramp. |

**Stop rule:** If no response after 5 touches over 14 days, move to Tier 2 nurture. Do not keep pushing. The account stays in the PQA pool and re-enters Tier 1 outreach when a new engagement trigger fires.

## Scenario 2: Tier 2 Nurture

**When to use:** The account is growing but not ready for sales engagement. PQA score is in the Nurture range (6-9), or the account was in Tier 1 but didn't engage after the first sequence. No dedicated sales time — these sequences run automatically, triggered by product behavior.

**Goal:** Accelerate the behavioral signals that would move the account from Tier 2 to Tier 1. Maintain awareness without consuming sales resources.

### Trigger-Specific Nurture Touches

These are automated sequences triggered by usage milestones — not calendar-cadenced. Each touch fires when the account hits a specific behavioral threshold.

**Trigger: Account crosses 50% of PQA threshold (growing toward Tier 1)**

> Subject: Teams like yours on [Product]

> Hi [Name],

> Your team's usage of [Product] has been growing steadily — you're now [specific: using X features, running Y workloads, growing Z% month-over-month].

> I wanted to share how similar teams are getting more value: [one specific customer example or use case that mirrors their usage pattern]. No ask here — just thought it might be useful as your team continues to expand.

> If you ever want to talk about what a committed relationship looks like at your scale, I'm [name] — feel free to reach out anytime.

**Trigger: New user from same domain joins (team spread signal)**

> Subject: Your growing team on [Product]

> Hi [Name],

> I noticed a new member of the [Company] team recently joined [Product]. As teams grow on the platform, a few things tend to come up — shared configurations, team-level administration, and making sure everyone's set up efficiently.

> Here's a quick guide to [feature that matters for teams: shared workspaces, team admin, etc.]: [link to resource]

> If your team's usage continues to grow, let me know — I can walk you through what team and enterprise plans look like whenever the timing is right.

**Trigger: Feature adoption deepens (sandbox → production, or new feature category)**

> Subject: Nice — your team is using [advanced feature]

> Hi [Name],

> I saw your team started using [specific feature] — that's one of the capabilities our more advanced accounts get the most value from.

> Here's a [case study / usage guide / best practices doc] for teams using [feature] at scale: [link]

> If you want to talk about how other teams at your scale are structured on [Product], I'm here.

### Nurture Cadence

These are not a fixed sequence — they fire independently based on behavioral triggers. An account might receive one nurture touch in three months or three in one month, depending on their usage trajectory.

**Re-evaluation rule:** When a Tier 2 account crosses the PQA threshold (score ≥ 10 on the Account Scoring Card), it automatically moves to Tier 1 and enters the harvest sequence. The nurture sequence stops, and the first touch sequence (Scenario 1) takes over.

## Scenario 3: Developer-to-Executive Bridge

**When to use:** The champion (practitioner) is engaged and supportive, but the conversation needs to reach the economic buyer (budget holder) to move toward commitment. This is the hardest transition in PLG and where most deals stall.

**Goal:** Connect practitioner-level value to executive-level priorities. Either arm the champion to carry the story upward (bottom-up) or reach the executive directly with usage data as credibility (top-down).

### Path A: Bottom-Up (Arm the Champion)

**When:** The champion is willing to advocate internally but doesn't have the materials or language to build the business case. This is the most common PLG path — almost every deal starts here.

**Talk track with the champion:**

"You've been using [Product] for [X months] and your team is running [specific workloads]. If you were going to bring this to [their VP/CTO/budget holder], what would they care about most — cost reduction, risk mitigation, infrastructure consolidation, or something else?"

*Listen. The champion's answer tells you which Stakeholder Framework dimension the executive operates in.*

"Here's what I can do — I'll put together a one-page executive brief that frames [Product] in terms of [what the champion said the executive cares about], using your team's actual usage data. You can bring that to [budget holder name] and see if they want to have a conversation with me directly, or we can set up a joint call."

**Materials to deploy (from the Champion Arming Kit):**

| Material | When to Send | What It Contains |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Usage summary | Immediately after this conversation | Team adoption metrics, feature utilization, growth trend — one page |
| Cost comparison | When champion confirms budget holder is aware | Current consumption vs. committed rate vs. cost of alternatives |
| Executive brief | When champion is ready to carry the story upward | Connects product value to executive's strategic priorities — business case in their language |

**Key principle:** Don't send all three at once. The usage summary opens the door. The cost comparison answers "what would this cost?" The executive brief translates practitioner value into executive language. Let the champion's internal process dictate the timing.

### Path B: Top-Down (Direct Executive Outreach)

**When:** The champion has confirmed the budget holder's name, or the usage data is strong enough to justify direct outreach. Use this when the product connects to something the executive already cares about (Tenet 5 Pattern 1 — top-down delegation).

**Email to the executive:**

> Subject: [Company] engineering team's usage of [Product]

> Hi [Executive Name],

> Your engineering team has been using [Product] in production for [X months] — [specific: Y users across Z use cases, growing at W% month-over-month].

> I'm reaching out because at this level of usage, most organizations benefit from a committed relationship — which typically means 15-25% savings versus pay-as-you-go, dedicated support with SLA guarantees, and enterprise-grade administration and security controls.

> I've worked with teams at [relevant reference customer or company type] through this same transition. Would 20 minutes make sense to discuss whether a committed agreement fits your infrastructure strategy?

**What makes this work:** The usage data is the credibility. You're not cold calling — you're informing an executive that their team has an established dependency on your product. The frame is: "your organization is already making this investment through consumption. I can help you get more value for less money."

### Path C: Event-Driven (Organizational Trigger)

**When:** Something inside the buyer's organization surfaces the product to executive or procurement attention — not because you triggered it, but because their internal processes made the product visible to a new stakeholder. Position yourself to be helpful in that moment.

**Trigger signals:** Invoice request from someone new (procurement is now involved), vendor questionnaire received (security review triggered), compliance audit mention from the champion, billing inquiry from finance (CFO or controller noticed the spend).

**Response frame:**

> Subject: Re: [Product] vendor documentation

> Hi [Name],

> I received the vendor questionnaire and wanted to make sure your team has everything you need to move quickly. Attached is our [SOC 2 / security documentation / compliance packet / standard contract].

> I've also put together a brief overview of how [Company]'s team has been using [Product] — [usage summary with key metrics]. This might be helpful context as your organization evaluates the vendor relationship.

> I work with our enterprise accounts and can be a resource through this process — happy to join a call with procurement, security, or whoever's leading the review.

**Key principle:** The event-driven touch is a service conversation, not a sales conversation. The organization's own process has surfaced the product — your job is to make it easy for the champion to navigate that process. Have the security packet ready before anyone asks. Make the champion look competent by giving them everything procurement needs before procurement requests it.

## Anti-Patterns

**"I'd love to introduce you to our platform."** They already know your platform. They use it every day.

**Leading with a demo offer.** They don't need a demo. The product is the demo. If they're in Tier 1, they've already validated the product through usage.

**Generic cadence timing (Day 1, Day 3, Day 7) without trigger logic.** PLG outreach should be triggered by behavioral signals, not calendar dates. The exception is the Tier 1 follow-up sequence, which runs on a fixed cadence because the initial outreach was already trigger-qualified.

**Sending all champion arming materials at once.** Each material maps to a buyer journey stage. Sending the executive brief at first touch is too aggressive. Sending the security packet during business case formation is premature. Match the material to the stage.

**Pushing for a meeting when the champion wants to go internal first.** PLG deals convert on the buyer's timeline. If the champion says "let me talk to my VP first," your job is to arm them for that conversation — not insist on being in the room.
