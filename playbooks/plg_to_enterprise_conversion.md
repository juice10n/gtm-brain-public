# 1. The Core Thesis

A PLG company has already solved the hardest problem in sales — getting people to use the product. The commercial engine isn't about generating demand. It's about recognizing demand that already exists in the usage data and converting it into structured, predictable revenue.

This changes the GTM leader's job description. In a sales-led motion, the work is: build the pipeline, run discovery, prove value, close the deal. In a PLG motion, the product has already built pipeline, the user's behavior is the discovery data, and value has been proven through usage. The work becomes: identify which accounts are ready for a sales conversation, help the buyer navigate from individual usage to organizational commitment, and convert variable consumption into committed revenue.

The principles underneath are the same — ICP clarity, buyer journey mapping, discovery sequencing, pricing conviction, metric discipline. The application is different because the product initiates the relationship, not the seller.

**The foundational operating principle: harvest before you hunt.** When the product has self-serve users, the warmest outbound pool already exists inside the product. Users who have activated, integrated, and expanded their usage are pre-qualified in ways no cold list can match. Reaching out to these users with an enterprise offer is not cold outbound — it is a fundamentally different motion. Product signals replace cold lists as top-of-funnel input. The discovery conversation starts at "I know this works" rather than "show me this works."

The pattern is well-documented at this point — Datadog, Slack, Figma, Atlassian, Snowflake all followed the same arc: the product builds the base, sales elevates it. OpenView's data shows that tracking product-qualified accounts increases the likelihood of fast growth by 61%, and McKinsey's 2023 research confirmed that the highest-performing PLG companies adopted a hybrid product-led sales motion. The question is no longer whether this works. The question is how to build the conversion engine — and that's what this playbook addresses.

# 2. The 17-Step Operating Sequence

This is the operator's playbook — what a GTM leader does, in order, when building the PLG-to-enterprise conversion engine. Each step has a specific deliverable and maps to one or more of the detailed reference sections that follow. The sequence is organized into three phases that mirror the Build-Operate-Transfer model.

## Phase 1: Build (Steps 1–12)

### Step 1: Product Truth
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (The Prerequisite)*

Understand what the product does today — in its current state, not aspirationally. Separately, understand the vision: where is the product going? Identify the gap between the self-serve experience and what an enterprise buyer would need. In PLG, product truth matters more than in sales-led because the product *is* the go-to-market motion. The self-serve experience defines the category positioning, the activation path, and the natural expansion vectors.

Key questions: What does the product do well right now? What enterprise requirements are missing (SSO, SLAs, data residency, audit logs, admin controls)? What's the Leading Indicator of Retention — the behavior that predicts a user will stay? Where is the product on the roadmap relative to what enterprise buyers will require?

**Deliverable:** Product truth document — current-state capabilities, enterprise readiness gaps, natural expansion vectors.

### Step 2: Stakeholder Framework
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (The Prerequisite)*

Run the Stakeholder Framework exercise for the enterprise buyer. Who are the personas in the enterprise buying committee? Map each using the standard six-column structure: persona, titles, business drivers, challenges, differentiators, and business outcomes. This exercise determines what "enterprise-ready" means for this specific product with these specific buyers. Without it, every subsequent step — scoring, segmentation, messaging, pricing — operates without a defined target.

#### Three Default PLG Personas

In PLG, three personas exist in almost every enterprise deal. Use these as starting points — most products will surface 3-5 total personas once the exercise is complete.

**Practitioner Champion** — the user who adopted the product. In PLG, this person is both the first customer and the first salesperson — they must advocate for the product internally before any sales rep gets involved. Titles vary by product (Engineer, Developer, Data Scientist, Designer, Analyst, Team Lead). Their business drivers are delivering technical work efficiently, evaluating and adopting better tools, and shipping faster. Their core challenge is not "should I use this?" — they already chose it — but "how do I get my organization to commit to this?" They struggle to navigate procurement, secure budget, and get organizational support for a tool they adopted individually. *PQA signal connection:* This persona is identified through product data — they're the primary user, the account creator, the person with the most usage. When the Account Scoring Card's "Champion identified" field is checked, it should be this person.

**Economic Buyer** — the budget holder who needs to approve the commitment. May never use the product directly. Titles: VP Engineering, CTO, Director of Infrastructure, Head of Data — whoever controls the budget for this type of tooling. Their business drivers are controlling infrastructure costs, reducing vendor sprawl, ensuring reliability, and maintaining security posture. Their core challenge is that teams adopt tools without organizational oversight (shadow IT risk) and consumption-based tools are hard to predict and budget. They need predictable spend that finance can plan against, not a credit card bill that changes every month. *PQA signal connection:* This persona is identified through enrichment, not product data. The Account Scoring Card's "Economic buyer reachable" field requires LinkedIn research or champion confirmation — the product analytics won't show this person directly.

**Procurement / Security Evaluator** — doesn't choose the product, approves or blocks it. Often surfaces late when spend crosses an organizational threshold. Titles: IT Security, Procurement Manager, Vendor Management, InfoSec, Compliance, Legal. Their business drivers are protecting the organization from vendor risk and ensuring compliance. Their core challenge is that self-serve tools lack the documentation (SOC 2, DPA, security questionnaire) that formal vendor approval requires. Having the security packet pre-built and proactively provided is the single most important tactical preparation for this persona — they are the most common deal-killer in PLG-to-enterprise conversion. *PQA signal connection:* This persona surfaces through financial signals on the Account Scoring Card — invoice requests, billing inquiries from someone other than the account creator, or vendor questionnaires received.

#### Facilitation Guide

The Stakeholder Framework is not a template to hand to a founder and say "fill this in." It is a facilitated exercise. The facilitator's role is to draw out thinking the founder doesn't know they have, challenge assumptions about who the buyer really is, and synthesize what they're hearing into the framework in real-time.

**Step 1: Start with Product Truth (15 min).** Before mapping personas, ground the exercise in what the product does today versus the vision. Key questions: What does the product do well right now? What enterprise requirements are missing? What's the gap between the self-serve experience and what an enterprise buyer would need?

**Step 2: List every person who touches the buying decision (10 min).** Start with the three defaults above and ask: "Who else would be involved?" Common additions: Finance/Controller (sees the bill, asks "what is this?"), Engineering Manager/Tech Lead (sits between champion and VP), Data/Platform Team (may own the vendor relationship in infrastructure products).

**Step 3: For each persona, work the six columns left to right (15 min per persona).** Each column constrains the next. Column 3 (business drivers): "What is this person accountable for? What gets them promoted?" Column 4 (challenges): push for specificity — "better how? What's the cost of the current approach?" Column 5 (differentiators): must map to Column 4 one-to-one. If a differentiator doesn't connect to a stated challenge, either the challenge is missing or the differentiator isn't real. Column 6 (business outcomes): state in the buyer's language. The test: could this persona say this sentence in a meeting with their boss?

**Step 4: Identify the connecting theme (10 min).** Look across all personas for the single narrative that holds the differentiated value together. In PLG, this often reveals the gap between how self-serve users think about the product (individual tool) and how the enterprise buyer needs to think about it (organizational capability). That gap is the positioning work and feeds directly into Step 3 (Category Creation).

**Common facilitation mistakes:** Accepting job titles as personas (VP Engineering is a title, not a persona — the persona is "Infrastructure Decision-Maker"). Skipping Column 3 and going straight to challenges (without knowing business drivers, you can't distinguish decision-driving challenges from annoyances). Filling in Column 5 before Column 4 is complete (the instinct to jump to "here's what our product does" produces features, not differentiators). Only mapping 1-2 personas (if you only map the champion, the conversion playbook is built for a single-threaded deal).

**Deliverable:** Completed Stakeholder Framework for PLG enterprise personas (minimum three personas, six columns each).

### Step 3: Category Creation (First Draft)
*Reference: Tenet 2 — Value Proposition & Positioning*

Draft the category position. The Stakeholder Framework produces a connecting theme across personas — the single narrative that holds the differentiated value together. That theme becomes the category claim. In PLG, this is particularly important because the self-serve product experience is the positioning. If the product sits in an existing category, the user already has a mental model. If you're creating a category, the in-product experience needs to teach the new framing without a human explaining it.

Hold this lightly. It's a hypothesis you'll test through the Tier 1 conversations in Steps 13–14. The gap between how self-serve users think about the product and how the enterprise buyer needs to think about it is a real tension in PLG.

**Deliverable:** First-draft category position and narrative arc.

### Step 4: Product Analytics Audit and Instrumentation
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (Signal Taxonomy)*

Audit what usage data exists and identify what's missing. You can't frame ICP from behavioral signals if the signals aren't being tracked. Common gaps in PLG companies: no domain-level aggregation (can't see account-level usage), no feature-depth tracking (can't distinguish sandbox from production), no team-spread visibility (can't see invitations or shared workspaces).

Pull the raw data: who are the top 50 accounts by spend? By growth rate? By number of users? Do these three lists overlap, or are they different populations? What does the sign-up → activation → retention funnel look like today? Where do users drop off?

**Deliverable:** Product analytics gap assessment, initial data pull of top accounts, activation funnel baseline.

### Step 5: Frame the ICP
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (From Stakeholder Framework to PQA Scoring, Signal Taxonomy)*

Combine three inputs into a single ICP frame: behavioral signals from the product data (usage velocity, breadth, depth, organizational, financial), persona titles and profiles from the Stakeholder Framework, and firmographic data (company size, industry, funding stage). This is the PQA model — the PLG equivalent of the ICP Scoring Model that sits downstream of the Stakeholder Framework.

*Terminology note: The more common term in PLG is PQL (Product-Qualified Lead), popularized by OpenView. This playbook uses PQA — Product-Qualified Account — because the unit of conversion is the account, not the individual user. Multiple users from the same corporate domain is the signal, not a single person's activation event.*

Calibrate by scoring retroactively: which accounts would you have wanted to talk to six months ago? Set initial scoring thresholds based on that exercise.

**Deliverable:** PQA scoring model v1 with signal weights calibrated to Stakeholder Framework personas.

### Step 6: Account Enrichment
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (The "Who's Behind the API Key" Problem)*

Identify the organizations behind the self-serve accounts and map them to the Stakeholder Framework personas. Start with minimum viable enrichment: sort by corporate email domain, identify team clusters, run manual LinkedIn research on the top 50 accounts to determine whether the economic buyer persona is reachable. This can be done by one person in a few days without tooling.

If manual enrichment changes which accounts you'd prioritize, invest in tooling (Clearbit, ZoomInfo) for automated reverse-lookup on sign-up domains. The enrichment layer is what makes the first touch feel like a warm continuation rather than a cold call.

**Deliverable:** Enriched account list with organizational mapping for top 50 accounts.

### Step 7: Segment into Tiers
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring (Three-Tier Segmentation, Negative PQA Signals)*

Segment the self-serve base into three tiers based on the PQA scoring model:

**Tier 1 — Harvest.** Accounts showing enterprise-ready behavioral signals where the Stakeholder Framework personas are reachable. Proactive sales outreach. 10–20 accounts in the first cohort.

**Tier 2 — Nurture.** Growing but not ready. Automated touches triggered by usage milestones. No dedicated sales time yet.

**Tier 3 — Watch.** Self-serve only. Monitor for signal changes. Some accounts stay here permanently — self-serve revenue is still revenue.

Apply the Negative PQA filter to suppress accounts that look active but don't match: hobbyists, tire-kickers, price-only buyers, single-use-case experimenters.

**Deliverable:** Tier 1 account list (10–20 accounts), Tier 2 and 3 classification, Negative PQA criteria documented.

### Step 8: Discovery and Qualification Framework
*Reference: Section 5 — Discovery in PLG*

Define what you need to learn quickly to determine if the inbound PLG user and their company are worth pursuing. The standard five-step discovery sequence adapts to PLG: Steps 1–3 (current state, stakeholders, pain) are partially answered by product analytics. Steps 4–5 (future vision, timeline/budget) are where the human conversation adds the most value.

Build the qualification criteria: use case fit, current-state cost (already known from consumption data), organizational readiness, champion influence, and differentiation fit. Define the kill criteria — what signals mean you walk away.

**Deliverable:** PLG-adapted discovery question framework, qualification criteria, kill criteria.

### Step 9: Pricing Tier Commitments
*Reference: Section 8 — Consumption-to-Committed Pricing*

Structure the commitment offer for Tier 1 accounts before beginning outreach. You need to know what you're offering before you start the conversation. Define: floor setting (70–80% of current run-rate), discount structure (15–25% off pay-as-you-go), commitment period (annual vs. quarterly), overage treatment (standard rates, not punitive), and which commitment structure fits (fixed allotment, minimum commit with true-up, or dedicated capacity).

Build the three-tier packaging: self-serve (unchanged), growth commit ($2K–$10K/month, 15–20% discount), enterprise commit ($50K+ ACV, 20–25% discount, dedicated support).

**Deliverable:** Pricing framework v1 with commitment structures, discount bands, and tier definitions.

### Step 10: Persona-Based Messaging for Tier 1
*Reference: Section 6 — Messaging: Champion to Executive*

Build the messaging for each Stakeholder Framework persona in the enterprise buying committee. The champion (practitioner) speaks in terms of time savings, workflow improvement, and output quality. The executive (budget holder) speaks in terms of cost reduction, risk mitigation, and headcount efficiency. The messaging must translate between the two.

Define the language shift: how the same product value gets expressed to the practitioner versus the executive. Build the outreach templates for the first touch, referencing what you know from product data.

**Deliverable:** Persona-specific messaging matrix, first touch templates for Tier 1 outreach.

### Step 11: Champion Arming Kit
*Reference: Section 6 — Messaging: Champion to Executive (Arming the Champion)*

Build the materials the champion needs to sell internally, staged to the buyer journey:

**Usage summary** — deploys at first touch when the PQA threshold is crossed. Shows the champion you've done your homework.

**Cost comparison** — deploys during business case formation when the champion asks "what do I bring to my VP?" Frames commitment as savings, not expense.

**Executive brief** — deploys when crossing the developer-to-executive bridge. Connects practitioner value to the executive's strategic priorities.

**Security and compliance packet** — deploys proactively at commitment stage or earlier if an event-driven trigger surfaces the product to organizational attention.

**Deliverable:** Four-piece champion arming kit, each mapped to a buyer journey trigger.

### Step 12: Outreach Sequencing and Workflows
*Reference: Section 3 — The PLG Buyer Journey (Engagement Triggers, The First Touch)*

Build the outreach sequences for Tier 1. Define which engagement trigger initiates which sequence: a billing inquiry gets a different first touch than a usage velocity signal. Build the CRM workflows that route PQA-qualified accounts to the right sequence automatically.

The first touch frame: "Your team is already using us. I'm reaching out because I help our larger accounts get more value — things like dedicated support, volume pricing, and custom configurations. Is that relevant for where you're headed?"

**Deliverable:** Outreach sequences for each engagement trigger, CRM routing workflows, first touch talk tracks.

## Phase 2: Operate (Step 13)

### Step 13: Execute First Tier 1 Cohort
*Reference: Section 7 — The PLG Sales Process*

Execute on the first 10–20 Tier 1 accounts and document everything. This is the Build-Operate-Transfer principle — operate the motion on a small cohort before systematizing anything.

Run the adapted discovery. Test the pricing conversation. Observe which engagement triggers produce the best conversations and which get ignored. Document every conversation: what worked, what didn't, what you learned about the buyer's process, how long each stage took, what materials the champion needed. Identify procurement patterns — what does the enterprise buying process look like for this product?

The learning from this cohort is more valuable than any pre-launch analysis. It refines Steps 5–12 retroactively.

**Deliverable:** First 5–10 enterprise conversations completed, documented learnings on engagement patterns, procurement insights, initial pipeline with stage tracking.

## Phase 3: Scale and Transfer (Steps 14–17)

### Step 14: Refine PQA Model, Pricing, and Messaging
*Reference: Section 4 — Account Scoring; Section 6 — Messaging; Section 8 — Pricing*

Feed the learnings from Step 13 back into the system. Which PQA signals predicted engagement? Which didn't? Adjust scoring thresholds. Which pricing framing landed? Which created friction? Update the commitment structures. Which messaging resonated with champions? With executives? Refine the language shift table and the champion arming materials.

This is the feedback loop that turns a hypothesis into a system. The PQA model, pricing, and messaging should all look different after the first cohort than they did before.

**Deliverable:** PQA scoring model v2, refined pricing framework, updated messaging and champion arming materials.

### Step 15: Define Pipeline Stages and Metrics Dashboard
*Reference: Section 9 — PLG Metrics & Pipeline*

Now that you've observed the motion in practice, define the CRM pipeline stages for PLG deals (S0–S6 adapted for PLG). Build the six-metric PLG dashboard: new sign-ups by source, activation rate, PQA volume, PQA-to-sales-engaged conversion, sales-engaged-to-committed conversion, and revenue mix (self-serve vs. committed). Set up pipeline arithmetic: required committed deals → required PQAs → required growing accounts.

Defining stages and metrics after the first cohort is deliberate — you're building from observed patterns, not hypothetical ones.

**Deliverable:** CRM stage definitions, PLG dashboard operational, pipeline arithmetic calibrated, blended forecasting model.

### Step 16: Build Tier 2 Nurture Sequences
*Reference: Section 3 — The PLG Buyer Journey*

Now that you know what Tier 1 conversion looks like, build the automated motion for Tier 2. Design sequences triggered by usage milestones — the signals that would move an account from Tier 2 to Tier 1. Build the content layer: what do growing accounts need to hear to accelerate their path to enterprise readiness?

These sequences run without dedicated sales time. They're product-signal-triggered, not calendar-cadenced.

**Deliverable:** Automated Tier 2 nurture sequences, usage milestone triggers, content assets for growing accounts.

### Step 17: Expansion Playbook
*Reference: Section 9 — PLG Metrics & Pipeline (Account Health Scoring, NRR)*

The committed deal is the beginning, not the end. Define the expansion motion: what signals indicate a new team, a new use case, or increasing usage beyond the current commitment? When do you trigger the next committed spend conversation? How do you move from one team to multiple teams within the same organization?

Build the account health scoring system (green/yellow/red) to surface risk before it becomes churn. NRR becomes the north star metric — if it's below 100%, adding new logos is filling a leaky bucket.

**Deliverable:** Expansion signal taxonomy, account health scoring framework, NRR tracking, expansion talk tracks.

# 3. The PLG Buyer Journey

*Primary reference: Tenet 3 — Buyer Journey & Sales Motion*

The PLG buyer journey is structurally different from a sales-led journey. In sales-led, the journey is: awareness → evaluation → technical validation → business validation → commitment. In PLG, the buyer starts at a different point. They've already self-selected, they already know the product works for their use case, and technical validation has happened through usage. The sales overlay enters at business validation.

This means the entire first half of the traditional sales motion — awareness, education, demo, pilot — is replaced by the product experience. The seller's job is to elevate the conversation from "this tool works" to "this should be an organizational commitment."

## The Buyer's Arc: Seven Stages

This is what the buyer experiences — the demand side of the conversion. The 17-step operating sequence describes what the operator builds; this describes what the buyer goes through.

**Stage 1: Individual Adoption** (Product-led, no sales involvement)
A developer, practitioner, or team lead finds the product and starts using it. The product experience is the demo. No seller is involved. Positioning must be embedded in the product itself — onboarding flows, empty states, upgrade prompts. The user understands the category within the first session without a sales conversation (Tenet 2). The product must demonstrate core value within minutes, not meetings.

**Stage 2: Team Spread** (Product-led, first sales-relevant signal)
Usage expands to multiple people within the same organization. This is the first meaningful signal. The product is solving a team-level problem, not just an individual one. Domain clustering (multiple users from the same corporate email domain), team invitations, and shared workspaces all indicate organizational adoption is beginning. Tenet 1 scoring identifies these accounts.

**Stage 3: Product-Qualified Account** (Tenet 1 scoring → Tenet 3 engagement trigger)
The account crosses the behavioral threshold that indicates sales-readiness. This is the PQA — the operational scoring layer that sits downstream of the Stakeholder Framework, prioritizing which self-serve accounts to engage first. The scoring model defines what "ready" looks like using usage velocity, breadth, depth, organizational, and financial signals — each calibrated against the enterprise personas identified in the Stakeholder Framework. The engagement trigger framework determines when and how a human enters the conversation.

**Stage 4: Sales Engagement** (Tenet 3 first touch → Tenet 4 adapted discovery)
The first human touch. This is not a cold call — it's an acknowledgment of a relationship that already exists. The rep arrives informed: they know the usage data, the feature adoption patterns, the team structure. Discovery picks up at Step 4 (future vision) of the standard five-step sequence because Steps 1-3 (current state, stakeholders, pain) have been answered by product analytics.

A critical sub-objective at this stage is triggering organizational awareness intentionally. Someone with budget authority needs to become aware of the usage — and this happens either top-down (CFO sees the bill, finance flags the spend) or bottom-up (the practitioner champion advocates to their manager). The seller's job is to make sure this awareness happens through the champion's advocacy, not accidentally through a billing surprise. Don't wait for the bill to trigger procurement — trigger the executive conversation proactively by arming the champion with what they need to carry the story upward.

**Stage 5: Business Case Formation** (Tenet 5 messaging → Tenet 3 business validation)
The conversation shifts from "we use this" to "should we commit to this?" The buyer needs to build the internal case. The seller's job is to arm the champion with executive-ready materials — connecting practitioner-level value to executive-level initiatives. The champion-to-executive messaging shift is the critical capability. Usage data becomes the business case.

**Stage 6: Committed Deal** (Tenet 8 pricing → Tenet 7 process)
Converting from consumption to committed spend. The pricing conversation is different — the buyer already knows what they're paying. The conversation isn't "here's what it costs" but "here's how to get more value for less money by committing." Procurement, contract terms, SLAs, enterprise requirements.

**Stage 7: Expansion** (Tenet 1 expansion signals → Tenet 8 tier upgrades)
The committed deal is the beginning, not the end. Expansion signals — new teams, new use cases, increasing usage — trigger the next cycle. NRR becomes the north star metric. In PLG, expansion revenue from existing users is the most efficient growth engine.

## Engagement Triggers

When does a self-serve account get a human touch? Triggers should be behavioral, not time-based:

- Spend crosses a threshold (e.g., $1K/month sustained for 60+ days)
- Usage growth rate exceeds a defined percentage month-over-month for 3+ consecutive months
- Multiple users from the same corporate domain
- Transition from development/sandbox to production workloads
- Billing inquiry or invoice request (signals procurement involvement)
- Support ticket from someone who isn't the original sign-up (signals team spread)

Each trigger routes to a specific outreach motion. A billing inquiry gets a different first touch than a usage velocity signal. The trigger taxonomy should be documented and automated through the product analytics layer.

## The First Touch

The outreach to a self-serve account is fundamentally different from cold outbound. You're not introducing yourself — you're acknowledging a relationship that already exists.

**The frame:** "Your team is already using us. I'm reaching out because I help our larger accounts get more value — things like dedicated support, volume pricing, and custom configurations. Is that relevant for where you're headed?"

This is not a traditional discovery call. The rep already has behavioral discovery from the product data. The call deepens understanding of organizational context — how the product fits into their stack, who else cares, what the roadmap looks like — not pain qualification. The pain is validated by their usage.

At Capsule, the same principle operated through a different mechanism. Before launch, 20,000 people joined the waitlist — a self-selected audience that had expressed interest before the product existed. The commercial challenge was identical: segment a warm pool, prioritize by fit signals, and convert interest into structured revenue. The waitlist became the first pipeline source — not through cold outbound, but through reaching out to people who had already raised their hand. Network seeding, the primary pipeline creation pattern at Capsule, shared the same logic: reach people who already have some relationship with you before reaching people who don't. PLG formalizes this principle with product signals as the data source.

## The Developer-to-Executive Bridge

The hardest transition in PLG is getting from the practitioner who loves the product to the executive who controls the budget. The Stakeholder Framework identifies both personas and maps their distinct challenges and business drivers. Three paths connect them — each maps to a specific persona relationship:

**Bottom-up (arm the champion).** Maps to the champion persona in the Stakeholder Framework. Give the developer or team lead the data to advocate internally — usage reports, ROI calculations, comparison to alternatives. Build executive-ready materials they can present without you in the room. The champion's challenges and business drivers (from the Stakeholder Framework) determine what materials they need and how to frame them. Gartner's research shows buyers spend only 17% of the purchase journey meeting with vendors — the champion must carry the story independently.

**Top-down (direct executive outreach).** Maps to the economic buyer persona in the Stakeholder Framework. Reach out to the VP Engineering or CTO directly, referencing the team's usage as the reason for contact. "Your engineering team has been using us in production for three months. I'd like to discuss whether a committed relationship makes sense." The executive's business drivers (from the Stakeholder Framework) determine the framing — infrastructure consolidation, cost reduction, vendor rationalization, competitive advantage. The usage data is your credibility — you're not cold calling, you're informing.

**Event-driven (organizational trigger).** Maps to the procurement, security, or finance persona in the Stakeholder Framework — whichever one surfaces the product to organizational attention. The bill gets big enough that finance notices. A security review triggers organizational awareness. A compliance requirement forces formal vendor evaluation. These are moments when the organization's own internal processes surface the product to executive attention — not because you triggered the conversation, but because something happened inside their company that made the product visible to a new stakeholder. Position yourself to be helpful in that moment rather than an obstacle. Have the security packet ready, the enterprise pricing structured, and make it easy for the champion to respond with everything procurement needs.

The Tenet 5 framework for executive escalation applies directly: Pattern 1 (top-down delegation) is when the product connects to something the executive already cares about — go straight to them. Pattern 2 (bottom-up business case) is when the product's value is practitioner-level and the executive gets involved later for approval. In PLG, almost every deal starts as Pattern 2. The playbook for turning Pattern 2 into Pattern 1 is finding the framing that makes a practitioner-level tool feel like an executive initiative — connecting usage data to the executive's strategic priorities (cost reduction, competitive advantage, infrastructure consolidation).

# 4. Account Scoring: The PQA Model

*Primary reference: Tenet 1 — ICP & Market Segmentation*
*Operating sequence reference: Steps 1–7*

In sales-led motions, ICP is defined by firmographics and use case fit, then validated through discovery conversations. In PLG, behavioral data from the product adds a powerful scoring layer — but it doesn't replace the foundational ICP work. The product can tell you who's active. It can't tell you who the right buyer is, what their buying committee looks like, or what business case will get the deal approved. That requires the same Tenet 1 methodology applied to any new company: Product Truth → Stakeholder Framework → scoring model.

## The Prerequisite: Product Truth and Stakeholder Framework

Before building the PQA model, the GTM leader must complete two foundational exercises:

**Product Truth.** What does the product do today versus the vision? In PLG, this matters more than in sales-led because the product *is* the go-to-market motion. The self-serve experience defines the category positioning, the activation path, and the natural expansion vectors. If there's a gap between the self-serve product experience and the enterprise value proposition — features that don't exist yet, integrations that aren't built, enterprise requirements (SSO, SLAs, data residency) that aren't met — the GTM leader needs to know that before building the conversion engine. Product truth determines which accounts *can* convert to enterprise today versus which ones require product investment first.

**Stakeholder Framework.** Who are the personas in the enterprise buying committee for this product? The developer who signed up is one persona. The VP Engineering or CTO who controls the budget is another. The procurement lead, the security reviewer, the finance team member who flags the bill — each is a distinct persona with different challenges, business drivers, and decision criteria. The Stakeholder Framework maps all of them using the standard six-column structure: persona, business drivers, challenges, differentiators, proof points, and outcomes.

This exercise determines what "enterprise-ready" means for this specific product with these specific buyers. Without it, the PQA model scores accounts on behavioral signals without knowing whether the right *people* are behind those signals. A developer burning $20K/month in inference is a strong behavioral signal — but if the Stakeholder Framework shows that the real buyer is the VP Engineering and they need infrastructure consolidation as the business driver, the PQA model must include signals that indicate that person exists and is reachable, not just that usage is high.

## From Stakeholder Framework to PQA Scoring

The PQA (Product-Qualified Account) model is the PLG equivalent of the ICP Scoring Model — the operational layer that sits downstream of the Stakeholder Framework and prioritizes which accounts to pursue first. The Stakeholder Framework answers the qualitative question ("who are we for and what do they care about?"). The PQA model answers the operational question ("of all the self-serve accounts, which ones match our enterprise ICP and are showing buying signals?").

## Signal Taxonomy

The Stakeholder Framework defines who the enterprise buyer is and what they care about. The signal taxonomy translates that into observable product behavior. For consumption and infrastructure products, five signal categories determine sales-readiness — each should be calibrated against the personas identified in the Stakeholder Framework:

**Usage velocity** — Growth rate over time matters more than absolute volume. An account spending $500/month but growing 40% month-over-month is more valuable than one spending $2,000/month flat. Sustained velocity over 3+ months separates real adoption from experimentation.

**Breadth signals** — Number of distinct users, number of distinct features or models used, API key proliferation within an org. Breadth indicates the product is solving multiple problems or serving multiple teams. A single power user is a fan. Multiple users across teams is an organization adopting.

**Depth signals** — Moving from sandbox to production, increasing usage sophistication, sustained usage rather than spiky experimentation. Depth indicates the product is embedded in real workflows, not being evaluated.

**Organizational signals** — Multiple users from the same corporate domain, team-level onboarding patterns, shared workspaces or projects. These signal the transition from individual tool to team infrastructure. Corporate email versus personal email is the simplest organizational signal.

**Financial signals** — Spend trajectory, approaching credit limits, billing inquiries, requesting invoices instead of using credit card payment. When someone asks for an invoice, procurement is involved. That's an enterprise signal.

## Three-Tier Segmentation

Not every active account deserves a sales conversation. Segment the self-serve base into three tiers defined by behavioral signals mapped to the Stakeholder Framework personas — not by company size alone:

**Tier 1 — Harvest.** Accounts showing enterprise-ready signals that align with the Stakeholder Framework personas. Proactive outreach. Multiple users from the same domain, production workloads, sustained spend growth, organizational signals present — and critically, evidence that the personas identified in the Stakeholder Framework are reachable within the account. A 50-person AI startup burning $20K/month in inference where you can identify the VP Engineering is Tier 1. A Fortune 500 with one developer experimenting and no path to the budget holder is not — even if usage signals are strong.

**Tier 2 — Nurture.** Growing but not ready. Lightweight automated touches — usage milestones, feature announcements, content about what teams like theirs are doing. The goal is to accelerate the behavioral signals that would move them to Tier 1. No dedicated sales time.

**Tier 3 — Watch.** Self-serve only. No sales involvement. Monitor for signal changes through automated scoring. Some accounts will remain self-serve permanently — and that's fine. Self-serve revenue is still revenue.

## The "Who's Behind the API Key" Problem

In PLG infrastructure, the user who signed up is rarely the buyer. The individual developer created the account; the VP Engineering or CTO controls the budget. Identifying the organization behind the self-serve user — and mapping it to the Stakeholder Framework personas — is a unique PLG challenge and one of the first practical blockers a GTM leader hits.

### Minimum Viable Enrichment (Week 1-2)

Start with what's free and immediately available. Corporate versus personal email domain is the simplest organizational signal — sort the user base by domain and you'll see team clusters immediately. Any user signing up with a @company.com email gives you the account identity for free. Users on Gmail, Yahoo, or other personal domains are likely hobbyists, students, or solo practitioners — flag them for the Negative PQA filter.

From the corporate domains, build a manual enrichment pass on the top 50 accounts by spend or growth rate. LinkedIn is the primary tool at this stage: search the company, identify who matches the Stakeholder Framework personas (the VP Engineering, the CTO, the team lead), and note whether the champion persona is reachable. This can be done by one person in a few days and doesn't require tooling investment.

### Tooling Investment (Month 2-3)

Once you've validated the enrichment matters — meaning the manual pass changed which accounts you'd prioritize — invest in tooling. Clearbit or ZoomInfo for reverse-lookup enrichment on sign-up domains. The integration should auto-enrich new sign-ups and backfill existing accounts with firmographic data (company size, industry, funding stage) and contact data (who else works there that matches your Stakeholder Framework personas).

The decision to invest in tooling should be data-driven: if manual enrichment on 50 accounts changed the Tier 1 list meaningfully (accounts you would have prioritized dropped out, accounts you would have missed moved in), the tooling pays for itself. If the manual pass confirmed what behavioral signals already showed, the tooling is nice-to-have, not essential.

### Ongoing Enrichment Signals

Beyond initial identification, monitor for organizational signals that emerge over time: multiple users from the same domain (team adoption in progress), billing changes from personal credit card to corporate card (procurement awareness), invoice requests (enterprise buying process initiated), and support tickets from someone who isn't the original sign-up (team spread confirmed). Each of these signals updates the account's PQA score and can trigger re-evaluation of tier placement.

The outreach frame changes fundamentally when enrichment is working. "Your team at Company X is already using us" is a different conversation than "would you like to try our product?" The enrichment layer is what makes the first touch feel like a warm continuation rather than a cold call.

## Negative PQA Signals

The PLG equivalent of the Negative ICP. Accounts that look active in the product data but don't map to the Stakeholder Framework personas — or match them on paper but lack the organizational conditions for an enterprise conversion:

- **Hobbyists and researchers** — high usage, no commercial intent. Often academics, students, or personal projects. Signal: personal email, no team spread, irregular usage patterns.
- **Tire-kickers** — spike of activity then flatline. Evaluated the product for a specific project and moved on. Signal: burst of usage followed by 30+ days of silence.
- **Price-only buyers** — already shopping competitors, no loyalty to the product. Signal: minimal feature adoption, pricing page visits, comparison searches in support docs.
- **Single-use-case experimenters** — using one narrow feature with no expansion path. The account will never grow beyond its current spend.

These patterns should be codified and automated. When an account matches negative PQA criteria, suppress it from sales routing. Every minute a rep spends on a hobbyist account is a minute not spent on a Tier 1 account.

The Capsule experience validates this principle at a different scale. Capsule's HubSpot loss taxonomy showed 35% of closed-lost deals were classified as "unqualified" — accounts that should never have entered the pipeline. In PLG, the equivalent waste is even larger because self-serve creates volume that dwarfs outbound. Automated disqualification is essential.

# 5. Discovery in PLG

*Primary reference: Tenet 4 — Discovery & Qualification*
*Operating sequence reference: Step 8*

The five-step discovery sequence transfers to PLG, but the first three steps change form:

**Step 1: Current state and workflow mapping** → Answered by product analytics. Which features are they using? What's the usage pattern? What integrations are active? The rep should arrive at the first conversation knowing the answer to "what are they doing with the product today?"

**Step 2: Stakeholder and process mapping** → Partially answered by product data. How many users? From which teams? Who signed up first? Who's the heaviest user? The human conversation fills in the organizational context: who's the budget holder, what's the decision-making structure, what other tools are in the stack?

**Step 3: Pain and friction** → Validated by usage itself. They're using the product because it solves a problem. The discovery question shifts from "what's your pain?" to "what would change if your entire organization had access to this, not just your team?"

**Step 4: Future vision** → This is where the human conversation adds the most value. "You're using this for X today. If we expanded to the whole engineering org, what does that look like? What would need to be true for that to happen?" Project a specific timeline and ask the prospect to validate it.

**Step 5: Timeline and budget** → "Your team is spending $X/month on consumption. At the rate you're growing, that's $Y annually. We can lock in a committed rate that saves you Z% — does that timeline work with your budget cycle?"

The critical adaptation: Steps 1-3 must be instrumented into the product analytics layer. If the rep can't infer current state, stakeholders, and pain from behavior data before the first call, the PQL handoff is blind. The difference between good and great PLG selling is whether the first conversation feels like a cold call or a warm continuation of an existing relationship.

The intentional curiosity philosophy from Tenet 4 still applies. Even with product data, the rep should be genuinely curious about the organizational context. "I can see your team has three active users running production workloads — help me understand how this fits into your infrastructure strategy" is curiosity informed by data. It builds trust because the buyer knows you've done your homework.

# 6. Messaging: Champion to Executive

*Primary reference: Tenet 5 — Messaging by Persona*
*Operating sequence reference: Steps 10–11*

In PLG, the champion is the practitioner who adopted the product. The executive is the budget holder who needs to approve the commitment. They speak different languages about the same product.

## The Language Shift

| Dimension | Champion (Practitioner) | Executive (Budget Holder) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Value frame | "This saves me time / makes my work better" | "This reduces cost / risk / headcount dependency" |
| Proof | Feature depth, ease of use, output quality | Usage data, ROI calculation, team productivity metrics |
| Concern | "Will this keep working as we scale?" | "What's the total cost of commitment vs. pay-as-you-go?" |
| Risk | Product capability gaps, integration complexity | Vendor stability, data security, compliance |
| Decision driver | Technical fit for their workflow | Business case vs. competing priorities |

The champion-to-executive messaging must translate the practitioner's experience into executive language. "This tool is great" becomes "this tool reduces our infrastructure cost by 30% while improving deployment speed." Usage data is the bridge — it's the proof the champion can carry to the executive without the seller in the room.

## Arming the Champion

Build the materials the champion needs to sell internally. Each piece maps to a specific stage of the buyer journey and should be deployed at the engagement trigger that matches:

- **Usage summary** — team adoption metrics, feature utilization, trend data. One page. Deploy at first touch (Stage 3 → 4 transition) when the engagement trigger fires. This is the opener — it shows the champion you've done your homework and gives them something concrete to share with their manager. Trigger: PQA threshold crossed or billing inquiry received.
- **Cost comparison** — current consumption cost vs. committed rate vs. cost of alternatives (building in-house, using competitors). Frame the commitment as savings, not expense. Deploy during business case formation (Stage 4 → 5 transition) when the champion needs to justify the spend internally. Trigger: champion confirms budget holder is aware and asks "what do I need to bring to my VP?"
- **Executive brief** — connects the product to the executive's strategic priorities. Not a product sheet — a business case in their language. Deploy when the developer-to-executive bridge is being crossed — either bottom-up (champion carrying it to the exec) or top-down (rep reaching out to the exec directly). This is the material that translates practitioner value into executive language. Trigger: champion identifies the budget holder by name or the rep initiates direct executive outreach.
- **Security and compliance packet** — pre-built, proactively provided. Don't wait for procurement to request it. Deploy at Stage 5 (commitment and structure) or earlier if an event-driven trigger surfaces the product to organizational attention through a security audit or compliance review. Trigger: any signal that procurement, legal, or security is now aware of the product — invoice request, vendor questionnaire received, or champion mentions an upcoming security review.

The sequencing matters. Leading with the executive brief at first touch is too aggressive — the champion hasn't validated whether they want to advocate yet. Leading with the security packet during business case formation is premature — the organization hasn't decided to commit. Match the material to the stage, and let the engagement trigger determine the timing.

Gartner found that customers who receive prescriptive advice and practical support are three times more likely to complete a high-quality purchase. Arming the champion is not a nice-to-have — it's the mechanism through which most PLG enterprise deals close, because the buying decision happens when the seller is not in the room.

# 7. The PLG Sales Process

*Primary reference: Tenet 7 — Sales Process & Playbook*
*Operating sequence reference: Step 13*

The standard seven-stage pipeline (S0-S6) adapts to PLG. The stages compress at the top and expand at the bottom:

**S0 (Prospecting) → Triggered by product data.** The PQA scoring model replaces SDR outreach as the pipeline entry point. When an account crosses the scoring threshold, it enters S0. The qualification gate is the same: ICP fit and confirmed need. But the data comes from usage, not a cold call.

**S1 (Discovery) → Informed by analytics.** The rep has behavioral discovery before the first conversation. S1 focuses on organizational context — who's the budget holder, what's the decision process, what's the strategic fit. Not "tell me about your business" but "I can see how your team uses us — help me understand the bigger picture."

**S2 (Scoping) → Expansion readiness, not evaluation design.** In sales-led, S2 designs the pilot or POC. In PLG, the user is already past evaluation. S2 confirms which use cases are in scope for an enterprise agreement, identifies additional teams or workflows, and surfaces the procurement path.

**S3 (Technical Validation) → Already happened.** The product is the POC. Technical validation occurred through usage. S3 becomes a checkpoint — are there enterprise requirements (SSO, SLA, data residency, compliance) that the self-serve product doesn't meet? If yes, address them. If no, advance.

**S4 (Business Case) → Built from usage data.** The business case writes itself when the buyer is already spending money. Frame it as: "You're currently spending $X on consumption. At your growth rate, that's $Y annually. A committed agreement at $Z saves you W% and adds dedicated support, SLA guarantees, and volume pricing." The business case is upgrading an existing relationship, not justifying a new expense.

**S5-S6 (Negotiation → Close) → Standard enterprise process.** Procurement, legal, contract terms. The same discipline applies — treat procurement as a separate sale, proactively provide security documentation, maintain champion engagement during the process.

# 8. Consumption-to-Committed Pricing

*Primary reference: Tenet 8 — Pricing & Packaging*
*Operating sequence reference: Step 9*

## The Hybrid Commit Model

The alignment sweet spot for PLG infrastructure companies: vendors get revenue predictability and upfront cash; customers get volume discounts, budget certainty, and expansion flexibility. This is where Snowflake, Datadog, MongoDB, and most modern infrastructure companies have landed.

**Floor setting.** Set the commit floor at 70-80% of the account's current run-rate. This makes the commitment a no-brainer — they're already spending this much. A floor at 100% of current usage feels like a trap. A floor at 70% feels like a discount with room to grow.

**Commitment period.** Match the buyer's planning horizon. Annual for enterprises with annual budget cycles. Quarterly for growth-stage companies that can't commit to 12-month forecasts. The discount scales with commitment length — annual gets a better rate than quarterly.

**Overages.** Bill at standard pay-as-you-go rates, not punitive rates. Punitive overages (2x commit rate) create resentment and incentivize customers to under-commit. Fair overages create natural upsell conversations — "you've exceeded your commit three months running, let's right-size your agreement."

**Discount structure.** The discount for commitment should be meaningful enough to justify procurement friction. 15-25% off pay-as-you-go pricing. Below 15%, the savings don't justify the effort of running a contract through legal. Above 25%, you're leaving revenue on the table.

## Three-Tier Packaging

**Self-serve (no commitment).** Pay-as-you-go, credit card, standard pricing. This is the top of funnel. Leave it alone. Don't try to force self-serve users into committed contracts before they're ready.

**Growth commit.** Minimum monthly commitment ($2K-$10K/month range), modest discount (15-20%), basic support SLA, invoice billing. Designed for the team-level buyer who wants predictability but doesn't need enterprise features. This is the bridge tier — the first step from consumption to committed.

**Enterprise commit.** Annual commitment ($50K+ ACV), significant discount (20-25%), dedicated support, custom SLAs, security/compliance documentation, dedicated account management. Designed for the organizational buyer.

## The Pricing Conversation

The pricing conversation with a self-serve user is different from a cold enterprise prospect. They already know what they're paying. The conversation isn't "here's what it costs" — it's "here's how to get more value for less money by committing."

The anchor isn't cost-of-alternative in the traditional sense — they're already using you. The anchor is cost-of-uncertainty: what happens when your usage doubles and you don't have a committed rate? What happens when you need enterprise support and don't have an SLA? What happens when your security team requires a vendor assessment and you're on a self-serve plan with no account manager?

Frame the commitment as an upgrade, not a lock-in. The buyer is getting something they don't have today — predictability, support, enterprise features — in exchange for a commitment they're functionally already making through their usage.

## Commitment Structures

Enterprise procurement needs a number. Three approaches to creating budget certainty for consumption products:

**Fixed allotment with rollover** (Twilio model). Buy a block of credits. Unused credits roll forward. Gives the buyer budget certainty and the vendor committed revenue. The rollover prevents the "use it or lose it" resentment.

**Minimum commit with true-up** (Snowflake model). Commit to a floor. Pay overage at standard rates. True-up quarterly. Snowflake had 168% NRR at IPO with this model — customers spent more as they grew, and the commit floor guaranteed a revenue base.

**Dedicated capacity** (GPU reservation model). For infrastructure products where the unit of value is compute capacity. The buyer reserves a fixed amount of capacity for a fixed period. Predictable for both sides. Works when usage is steady and the buyer values guaranteed availability.

# 9. PLG Metrics & Pipeline

*Primary reference: Tenet 9 — Metrics & Pipeline Hygiene*
*Operating sequence reference: Steps 15, 17*

## The PLG Conversion Funnel

The traditional pipeline (meetings → discovery → SQO → proposal → close) doesn't capture the PLG motion. The PLG funnel is:

**Sign-ups → Activated accounts → Growing accounts → PQAs → Sales-engaged → SQO → Committed deal**

Each stage has a conversion rate that becomes a health metric. The key insight: in PLG, the top-of-funnel metrics are product metrics (activation rate, usage growth), not sales metrics (meetings booked). The handoff point — PQA to sales-engaged — is where the product funnel becomes a sales funnel.

## PLG Dashboard: Six Metrics

Alongside the standard Weekly CEO Dashboard, a PLG company needs six additional metrics:

| # | Metric | What It Tells You | Primary Data Source | Update Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | **New sign-ups by source and segment** | Where are users coming from? Which sources produce users that convert? | Product analytics platform (Amplitude, Mixpanel, Heap) + UTM/attribution data | Daily |
| 2 | **Activation rate** | Sign-up to meaningful first usage. Definition of "meaningful" should be tied to the behavior that predicts retention (the Leading Indicator of Retention from Tenet 1). | Product analytics platform — requires a defined activation event instrumented in the product | Weekly |
| 3 | **PQA volume and growth** | Accounts crossing the scoring threshold. Is the product funnel producing enough sales-ready accounts? | Data warehouse (scoring model query combining product analytics + enrichment data + billing) or product analytics with custom scoring | Weekly |
| 4 | **PQA-to-sales-engaged conversion rate** | Are sales reps successfully initiating conversations with PQAs? Low conversion here means the first touch isn't working or the PQA definition is too generous. | CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot) — requires PQA-qualified accounts to be routed into CRM with stage tracking | Weekly |
| 5 | **Sales-engaged-to-committed conversion rate** | Are conversations converting to committed deals? Low conversion here means the pricing conversation, business case, or procurement navigation needs work. | CRM — deal stages from sales-engaged through closed-won | Weekly |
| 6 | **Revenue mix** | Self-serve consumption vs. committed ARR. The "predictability ratio." Track progress toward a more forecastable revenue base. | Billing system (Stripe, Chargebee) + CRM (for committed contract values) | Monthly |

This dashboard tells you three things: whether the product funnel is healthy (metrics 1-3), whether the sales overlay is converting (metrics 4-5), and whether the revenue base is becoming more predictable (metric 6). The critical integration requirement: product analytics, billing, enrichment, and CRM must be connected — typically through the data warehouse — for metrics 3-6 to function. If these systems are siloed, building the data pipeline is a Week 1 priority.

## Consumption-Specific Revenue Metrics

**Net dollar retention (NDR/NRR)** becomes the most important metric. It tells you whether accounts are growing, flat, or contracting. In PLG, NRR above 100% means the existing base functions as a growth engine. NRR below 100% means you have a leaky bucket that no amount of new logo acquisition can fill.

**Logo churn vs. revenue churn.** Critical separation in consumption models. You can retain logos but lose revenue if usage declines. A customer that drops from $10K/month to $2K/month hasn't churned — but the revenue impact is worse than a $2K/month customer churning entirely.

**Expansion revenue vs. new logo revenue.** Track the ratio. Healthy PLG companies derive 30-50%+ of new ARR from expansion. If new logo revenue dominates, the conversion motion isn't working or the product doesn't expand within accounts.

**Committed ARR as a percentage of total revenue.** The predictability ratio. Track this quarterly. A company with 80% committed ARR can forecast with confidence. A company with 20% committed ARR is running on consumption uncertainty. The sales overlay's job is to move this ratio up over time.

## Pipeline Arithmetic for PLG

The backward math changes. Instead of: target ÷ close rate = required pipeline = required SQOs = required meetings, it becomes:

**Target = committed ARR renewals + committed ARR new + consumption growth**

The "new committed ARR" has its own pipeline arithmetic:

- Required committed deals = target ÷ average commit size
- Required PQAs = committed deals ÷ PQA-to-close rate
- Required growing accounts = PQAs ÷ growth-to-PQA rate

**Worked example:** Target is $2M in new committed ARR. Average commit size is $50K. PQA-to-close rate is 25%. Growth-to-PQA rate is 15%.

- Required committed deals = $2M ÷ $50K = 40 deals
- Required PQAs = 40 ÷ 0.25 = 160 PQAs entering the sales funnel
- Required growing accounts = 160 ÷ 0.15 = ~1,067 accounts showing growth signals

This tells you three things immediately. First, if the self-serve base doesn't produce 1,067 growing accounts per year (~89/month), the product funnel is the bottleneck — no amount of sales execution fixes insufficient adoption. Second, if PQA volume is strong but close rate is below 25%, the problem is downstream: pricing conversation, business case strength, or procurement navigation. Third, the average commit size is a lever — moving from $50K to $75K average commit cuts the required deals from 40 to 27 and the required PQAs from 160 to 107. Packaging and pricing tier design directly impacts pipeline arithmetic.

## Forecasting Blended Revenue

Three components:

1. **Committed ARR** — High confidence, contracted. Forecasts like traditional pipeline.
2. **Consumption above commit** — Moderate confidence. Based on historical overage patterns with a trailing 3-month trend applied.
3. **Self-serve consumption** — Variable. Modeled on trailing 3-month trends with cohort analysis. Most volatile, lowest confidence.

The three-tier forecasting structure from Tenet 9 still applies, but the inputs are different. Committed deals forecast like traditional pipeline (commit/best case/upside). Consumption forecasts like a product metric (cohort-based, trend-based).

## Account Health Scoring

A dashboard view that surfaces risk before it becomes churn:

- **Green** — Growing usage, expanding team, approaching or exceeding commit threshold. Expansion opportunity.
- **Yellow** — Flat usage, stable but no expansion signal. Retention risk if a competitor enters.
- **Red** — Declining usage, contraction risk. Proactive outreach to understand what changed.

This is the consumption equivalent of deal stage-time tracking from Tenet 9. It surfaces risk early enough to intervene.

# 10. The 90-Day Implementation Calendar

The 17-step operating sequence mapped to a 90-day timeline. The sequencing follows the same principle as Tenet 3's six-step motion build: diagnose before installing, build from what's working, prove the model before scaling.

## Days 1–30: Build the Foundation (Steps 1–7)

**Week 1: Steps 1–2 — Product Truth, Analytics Audit, and Stakeholder Framework.**

Product Truth and Analytics Audit (Days 1–5): For a company that's 95% inbound/self-serve, the analytics audit is the single most important exercise in the first week. The product data is the pipeline — if you can't see it, you can't work it.

- Sit with the product and engineering team. Document current-state capabilities, enterprise readiness gaps, and the natural expansion vectors.
- Audit the product analytics layer systematically. For each question below, determine whether the data exists, where it lives, and what format it's in:
  - **Domain-level aggregation:** Can you see account-level usage, or only individual user activity? Can you group users by corporate email domain? If sign-ups are on personal email (Gmail, Yahoo), is there any mechanism to identify the organization behind them?
  - **Activation funnel:** What is the sign-up → first meaningful usage → sustained usage path? Where do users drop off? What's the current activation rate? Is there a defined activation event, or is it ambiguous?
  - **Feature-depth tracking:** Can you distinguish between sandbox/experimentation usage and production workloads? Can you see which features each account uses and how usage sophistication changes over time?
  - **Team-spread visibility:** Can you see team invitations, shared workspaces, or multi-user activity within a single account? Is there any visibility into how usage spreads from the initial sign-up to additional users?
  - **Financial data accessibility:** Can you pull spend by account over time? Can you see growth rate, not just current spend? Is billing data connected to product usage data, or are they in separate systems?
  - **Data infrastructure:** Where does each data source live — product database, analytics platform (Amplitude, Mixpanel, Heap), data warehouse (Snowflake, BigQuery, Redshift), billing system (Stripe, Chargebee), CRM? What's piped together and what's siloed?
- Pull the raw data: top 50 accounts by spend, by growth rate, and by user count. Note whether these three lists overlap or diverge — overlap means a clear Tier 1 cohort; divergence means signal calibration matters more.
- Map the sign-up → activation → retention funnel with actual numbers. Define the Leading Indicator of Retention — the specific behavior that predicts a user will still be active at 90 days.
- Run the Stakeholder Framework exercise for enterprise personas. Start with the three PLG defaults: practitioner champion, economic buyer, procurement/security evaluator.

**Week 2: Steps 3–5 — Category, Instrumentation, ICP.**
- Draft the category position based on the connecting theme across Stakeholder Framework personas.
- Close the product analytics gaps identified in Week 1. Prioritize by impact: domain-level aggregation and spend-by-account are prerequisites for the PQA model — without them, scoring is impossible. Feature-depth and team-spread are important but can be instrumented in parallel. Get engineering to commit to instrumentation timelines for each gap.
- Build the PQA scoring model: define signal weights (velocity, breadth, depth, organizational, financial) calibrated against the Stakeholder Framework personas. Retroactively score the top 50 accounts. If data gaps prevent full scoring, score on available signals and note which dimensions are blind spots.

**Week 3: Steps 6–7 — Enrichment and Segmentation.**
- Run manual enrichment on the top 50 accounts. LinkedIn research to identify whether Stakeholder Framework personas are reachable.
- Segment the self-serve base into Harvest / Nurture / Watch. Identify the first Tier 1 cohort (10–20 accounts).
- Apply the Negative PQA filter: suppress hobbyists, tire-kickers, price-only buyers, single-use-case experimenters.

**Week 4: Prepare for outreach.**
- Map the existing conversion path: how do accounts upgrade today? What friction exists?
- Begin building the deliverables needed for Steps 8–12 (discovery framework, pricing, messaging, champion arming kit, outreach sequences).

**Deliverables by Day 30:** Product truth document, completed Stakeholder Framework, PQA scoring model v1, Tier 1 account list (10–20 accounts), product analytics gap assessment, initial conversion funnel metrics, first-draft category position.

## Days 31–60: Build the Motion and Begin Execution (Steps 8–13)

**Week 5–6: Steps 8–11 — Discovery, Pricing, Messaging, Champion Kit.**
- Finalize the PLG-adapted discovery framework: what questions to ask, what qualification criteria to apply, what kill criteria to enforce.
- Structure the commitment offer: floor setting, discount bands, commitment structures. Build the three-tier packaging.
- Build persona-specific messaging for Tier 1: champion language, executive language, first touch templates.
- Build the champion arming kit: usage summary template, cost comparison framework, executive brief template, security packet.

**Week 7–8: Steps 12–13 — Sequences and First Cohort.**
- Build outreach sequences for each engagement trigger. Set up CRM routing workflows.
- Begin outreach to Tier 1 accounts. Run adapted discovery. Test the pricing conversation.
- Document every conversation: what worked, what didn't, what you learned about the buyer's process.
- Identify procurement patterns: what does the enterprise buying process look like for this product?

**Deliverables by Day 60:** Discovery framework, pricing framework v1, persona messaging, champion arming kit, first 5–10 enterprise conversations completed, initial pipeline with stage tracking.

## Days 61–90: Refine and Scale (Steps 14–17)

**Week 9–10: Step 14 — Refine Everything.**
- Feed learnings from the first cohort back into the PQA model: adjust scoring thresholds, update signal weights.
- Refine pricing based on actual conversations: did the floor land? Did the discount motivate commitment?
- Update messaging and champion arming materials based on what champions actually needed.
- Close the first committed deals from the Tier 1 cohort.

**Week 11–12: Steps 15–17 — Metrics, Nurture, Expansion.**
- Define CRM pipeline stages for PLG deals. Build the six-metric PLG dashboard.
- Calibrate pipeline arithmetic: required PQAs → required growing accounts → required sign-ups.
- Launch automated Tier 2 nurture sequences triggered by usage milestones.
- Define expansion signals and build the account health scoring framework (green/yellow/red).

**Deliverables by Day 90:** First committed deals closed, documented conversion playbook, PLG dashboard operational, Tier 2 nurture sequences live, pipeline arithmetic calibrated, expansion signal taxonomy defined.

# 11. The PLG Sales Hire

*Primary reference: Tenet 10 — Enablement & Onboarding*

The PLG sales role is a different job than the traditional enterprise AE. Hiring the wrong profile is one of the most common and most expensive mistakes in PLG-to-enterprise conversion. The failure mode is predictable: a company hires reps who are excellent at running demos and cold outreach, then watches them struggle with accounts that already use the product and don't need a demo.

## The Profile

**Product sense over demo skills.** The PLG seller needs to understand the product deeply enough to read usage data and infer what the customer is trying to accomplish. They should be able to look at an account's consumption pattern and form a hypothesis about their workflow, their pain, and their expansion potential before the first conversation. This is a fundamentally different skill than building a compelling demo narrative.

**Data literacy.** The PLG seller's discovery starts in the product analytics dashboard, not on a cold call. They need to be comfortable interpreting usage trends, spend trajectories, feature adoption patterns, and team-spread signals. They should be able to translate "this account's API calls increased 40% month-over-month across three distinct use cases" into a conversation about enterprise commitment.

**Business case building over objection handling.** The traditional enterprise AE spends significant energy on competitive objections and proving value. The PLG seller rarely faces either — the user already chose the product, and value has been proven through usage. The skill that matters is building the business case: helping the champion translate practitioner-level value into executive-level justification. This requires financial fluency — understanding ROI calculations, cost-of-alternative framing, and how to position a commitment as cost reduction rather than a new expense.

**Consultative patience over closing urgency.** PLG deals convert on the buyer's timeline, not the seller's. The practitioner champion needs time to build internal support, get budget approval, and navigate procurement. Reps who push for close before the internal buying process is ready will damage the champion relationship and lose deals that would have closed with patience. The best PLG sellers act like trusted advisors who make the champion's internal advocacy easier — not closers trying to manufacture urgency.

**Technical credibility.** Especially in infrastructure and developer-facing products, the seller must be credible in the buyer's technical domain. They don't need to be an engineer, but they need to speak the language fluently enough that the practitioner champion doesn't feel like they're being "sold to" by someone who doesn't understand their world. The seller who can say "I noticed you're running production workloads across three clusters — are you thinking about consolidating?" earns instant credibility.

## Interview Signals

When evaluating PLG sales candidates, probe for these:

- Ask them to describe a deal where the customer was already using the product before sales engaged. How did they approach the first conversation? What did they learn from the usage data before reaching out?
- Ask how they've built a business case for an existing user to move to a committed contract. What materials did they create? How did they arm the champion?
- Ask about a deal where they had to bridge from a practitioner champion to an executive budget holder. What was the framing shift? How did they translate technical value into business value?
- Ask them to interpret a hypothetical usage dashboard — give them consumption data and ask what they'd infer about the account and how they'd approach the conversation.

Candidates who default to demo-centric language ("I'd show them…"), cold-outbound war stories, or aggressive close techniques are the wrong profile. Candidates who talk about reading usage data, building business cases, and arming champions are the right profile.

## Ramp Model

The PLG sales ramp is shorter on product training and longer on data fluency. Week 1–2: product immersion — use the product, understand the analytics dashboard, read the top 20 accounts' usage data. Week 3–4: shadow existing conversations (if any), study the PQA scoring model, learn the Stakeholder Framework personas. Week 5–8: first outreach to Tier 1 accounts with coaching. The leading indicator of ramp success is whether the rep's first conversations reference specific usage data, not whether they can deliver a polished product demo.

# 12. Common Failure Modes

**Skipping the Stakeholder Framework and going straight to behavioral scoring.** Usage data tells you which accounts are active. It doesn't tell you who the enterprise buyer is, what their buying committee looks like, or what business case gets the deal approved. Building the PQA model without first running the Stakeholder Framework is scoring accounts against signals you haven't validated. You'll prioritize accounts with strong usage where no budget holder is reachable and miss accounts with moderate usage where the VP is already looking for a solution like yours.

**Treating self-serve users like cold prospects.** They already chose you. The outreach can't sound like a cold email. Every touch should reference what you know about their usage. "I noticed your team…" not "I'd love to introduce you to…"

**Building outbound before harvesting inbound.** You have warm accounts using the product right now. Why go cold? Exhaust the Tier 1 cohort before investing in cold outbound. Cold outbound in PLG targets accounts matching the enterprise ICP that haven't yet entered the product — it's the last pipeline layer, not the first.

**Pricing the commitment too aggressively.** The floor should be a no-brainer — 70-80% of current run-rate. If the buyer has to agonize over whether the commitment makes sense, you've set the floor too high. You want the conversation to be "of course we'll commit to that — we're already spending more."

**Over-engineering the motion before you have signal.** Start with 10 accounts, not a playbook for 1,000. The first cohort teaches you everything — which signals predict engagement, what the pricing conversation sounds like, how the procurement process works. Build the system from observed patterns, not hypothetical ones. This is the Build-Operate-Transfer model applied to PLG.

**Skipping the product analytics instrumentation.** If you can't see who's using the product, how they're using it, and how usage is changing, you're flying blind. Invest in the data layer before the outreach layer. The PQA model is only as good as the data underneath it.

**Hiring traditional enterprise reps for PLG.** The PLG sales role is different. It requires product sense, data literacy, and business case building — not demo skills and cold calling muscle. The seller who arrives at a conversation already understanding the buyer's usage and speaks their technical language outperforms the seller who asks "tell me about your business."

**Ignoring NRR in favor of new logos.** In PLG, the most efficient growth engine is expansion within the existing base. If NRR is below 100%, adding new logos is filling a leaky bucket. Fix retention and expansion first. Then scale acquisition.
